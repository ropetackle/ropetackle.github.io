Thomas Marshall In 1914

Relationship: Thomas Marshall is a Great-Great-Grandfather of Chloe and Daniel Marshall.
 
Born: 1881 in Nottingham
 
Parents: Thomas Marshall and Elizabeth Russell
 
Brothers and Sisters: George,  Mary,  William,  Thirza,  Elizabeth,  Charlotte,  Annie
 
Wife: Florence Pepper
 
Children: Thomas Robert (Tommy)
 
Died: 1941 in Glasgow

Thomas Marshall, who was the third child of Thomas Marshall, a Bricklayer, and his wife Elizabeth Russell, was born in Nottingham on 12th April 1881

He had two brothers and five sisters. These were:-

George, Mary, William, Thirza, Elizabeth, Charlotte, and Annie

The first reference to Thomas is his Birth Certificate. When he was born his parents were living at 5 Potts Square, Pierrepont Street in the Parish of St Mary’s, Nottingham.
It is interesting to note that when she registered his birth his mother did not sign the Register but "made her mark" with an "X". this would seem to indicate that she could not read or write.

When the 1891 Census was taken he was 10 years old. By then the family had grown and Thomas had another brother and three more sisters. The family had moved from Potts Square to a 4 roomed house at 6 Skelton Terrace, Hawthorne Street, Wilford, Nottingham.
Wilford is about 2 miles South of the centre of Nottingham on what is now the A6514. 
Conditions in the house, which appears to have been a typical C19th working class house, probably a “2 up and 2 down”, must have been crowded. In addition to the 9 members of the Marshall family there was also a 16 year old lodger, Samuel Featherstone, a Coal Mine Pit Boy.

By the time of the 1901 Census Thomas was 20 years old and, like his father was working as a Bricklayer. He was still living with his parents and two brothers and five sisters and was probably working with his father. They were living at 6 Cary Cottages, Hawthorne Street, Wilford, Nottingham.
Whether 6 Cary Cottages and not 6 Skelton Terrace were the same house is not known but it is thought that they could have been. The fact that the numbers are the same and that the 1891 Census Return does not make any mention of any Cary Cottages in Hawthorne Street points in this direction.

It was on the 10th August of that year that he made the momentous decision to change his profession. He enlisted in the Army for 7 years.
