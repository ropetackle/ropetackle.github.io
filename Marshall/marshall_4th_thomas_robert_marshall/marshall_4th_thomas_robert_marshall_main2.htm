After Tommy and Peggy Davies were married at Rainhill Parish Church on 19th June 1937 they set up home in Hillington, Glasgow at 50 Gladsmuir Road. There home was a couple of streets away from where his parents lived in Bearford Drive.

It was there that their first child, Robin was born on 1st February 1939.

Later that year, because of the imminent threat of war, Tommy was called back to St Helens and he and his family returned there. They went to live at 4 The Grove, Bleak Hill. It was there that his other two sons were born, Alan on 29th December 1941 and Brian on 12th August 1944.

Tommy realised that as the war went on he would be conscripted into one of the Armed Services and therefore volunteered for the RAF to train as a Medical Orderly. However when he had finished his basic training he was "asked" to "volunteer" to become a Radio Operator. At the time no one knew what this involved. There were Radio Telegraphists who used Morse Code to communicate with aircraft, and Wireless Operators who used verbal communication. What a Radio Operator did was then "Top Secret", even the volunteers did not know what they had volunteered for! It eventually turned out that the title of a Radio Operator was a cover name for what was subsequently known as a Radar Operator, Radar having been just invented and very much considered as being a "Secret Weapon" in Britain's defence plans.

After training as a Radar Operator he was posted to a Radar Station in Scotland at Corsewall Point on the North West tip of Loch Ryan, Galloway.

From what he said, life there was very comfortable. He lived in lodgings in Kirkcolm and for a period his wife and son went to stay with him in his lodgings.

While in the RAF Tommy suffered from appendicitis and went to hospital to have his appendix removed. He says that his medical records were confused with those of another serviceman who had an incurable fatal illness. As a result he was discharged from the RAF as medically unfit - he often wondered what happened to the other man!

Upon his discharge from the RAF he returned to Pilkington Brothers in St Helens and worked in their Work Study Department. Subsequently he joined their Industrial Relations Department and eventually became the Chief Industrial Relations Officer for the whole Pilkington Group.

After the War had ended Tommy and his family moved to 203 Dentons Green Lane and they lived there until he moved to 117B Higher Lane, Rainford in the 1960s. He lived at Rainford until his death.

Thomas died unexpectedly, aged 58, on 14th July 1970. He had been feeling ill at work and was being driven home when he suffered a major heart attack and collapsed and died in the car.
