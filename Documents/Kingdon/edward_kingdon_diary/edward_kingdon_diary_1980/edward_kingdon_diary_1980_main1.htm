March 3rd

Noticed last entry was Oct. 1978. Have never kept a diary. Must try. [He had actually started another diary on 21st December 1978.]

March 8th

Letter from Jane. Upset to read that she had been robbed in Paris of £80. Judging from the tone of the letter did not allow it to completely spoil her week-end. She is very resilient.

March 10th

Am summoned to the aid of Mrs Clifford, aged 84, Audrey Sterry’s mother. A blackbird had come down the chimney. Easily caught it in my two hands and set it free. Returned home two hours later completely exhausted, having been kept standing whilst listening to a monologue from this tough old lady. Two hours standing and one hour 59 minutes talking and still as bright as a button. Maiden name Taylor, supervisor in a munitions factory in 1914 – 18 war and nick named Purity by the girls she supervised. Purity Taylor will be our name for her from now on.

Slowly recovering aided by a stiff whisky when Ricky and Phil called. Took us out for a drink – the merry brother bachelor 58, married divorcee 43 and very successful marriage. Invited to Chipping Sodbury to see film he took of Mr Sawyer and me at the Toll Gate, Chadwell Heath in early 1960’s.

March 15th

Finished Trollope’s Barchester Towers. Enjoyed it immensely. Can not remember even a mention of him when I was a schoolboy. Am convinced I would not have appreciated him when younger. Compensations in being a late developer.

March 24th

Cheltenham to buy a cable clip for car heater. None in stock. Next week perhaps?

March 26th

Almost unbearable pain in both upper arms and neck, lasted minutes.

March 31st

Since 26th have been resting. Bed every afternoon. Slight twinges but chest still feels raw and sore.

Since Helen’s birthday a cold, wet dull month. Have sown a row of peas and broad beans early March. Barely half the Autumn sown beans have survived the winter and then two very hard frosts on successive nights have decimated the remainder.

Put some runner beans in pots in the cold frame.

First daffodils appeared on March 10th in the shelter of a lavender bush. But the rest of the daffodils still to flower.

Mum had good news on 21st. doctor checked blood pressure and it has now returned to normal but continue with the pills- moduretic.

On 28th a letter from Joy and Albert. Glad to hear that Jane had written to them, in reply to birthday present I assume. Joy’s words “ a lovely letter, talent for writing an interesting letter, we were very pleased”.

April

April 1st

Milder but no sunshine. This quote from Trollope’s Dr Thorne p.625 puts paid to my belief that Springs were better when I was young. Written in 1858:-

“The comic almanacs give us dreadful pictures of January and February: but, in truth the months which should be made to look gloomy in England are March and April. Let no man boast himself that he has got through the perils of winter till at least the seventh of May”.

April 3rd

Birthday letter to Violet enclosing £5 premium Bond.

April 4th

Severe frosts last two nights but sun during day.

Last night watched a very interesting programme on dyslexia; the probable reason for my mother’s inability to read or write. Made several attempts to teach her when I was between the ages of 12 and 15, but she seemed to be unable to recognise the letters even, the lesson usually ending in tears, distressing for both of us.

My mother never spoke of her early days and I never asked any questions. That she had had small pox, was a delicate child, that her father was a Master Tailor – very proud of this – keeping a shop in Coinage Hall Street?, Helston, and that she had two sisters Janie – Olive’s mother, and Katie the youngest – Violet’s mother, and a brother Jack who was drowned at sea is about the total of my factual knowledge. Jack’s photo in what I assume to be a midshipman’s uniform and looking very young was always on display in our sitting room, in which we rarely sat. I assume it was because her brother was called Jack that she always called me so, although I was christened John. I was curious about this when young but never asked why.

My Grandfather died before I was born, name Smith, small in stature. Recollection of my Grandmother, tall and angular with a strong personality. She died when I was about 9 or 10. My mother and I went down from Newton Abbot to Helston for the funeral. The funeral was over when we got to Auntie Katie’s house where the returning mourners had assembled. My mother cried and I said in a loud voice “It is no use crying over spilt milk”. The embarrassed silence and the queer looks the assembled adults gave me was overwhelming and I fled to the shelter of the privy in the courtyard.

Ian, 5 ft. 9 ½ in., cut the grass and prepared the stakes for the runner beans – very helpful. Wish Jane had been here as well but obviously Andy has priority. They are hoping to come before the end of March. [April?.]

April 7th

Had a wonderful evening. Helen and I stayed up till 3 am. Felt very close to her. I’ll treasure those hours together.

April 8th

Robin took us all into Cheltenham. Enjoy being chauffeur driven. Mum and I did a bit of food shopping whilst Helen, Robin and Ian dashed around doing theirs. Helen bought a pair of sandals to walk in the footsteps of Sir Walter Scott in July. Met in the Star for a drink.

April 9th

Helen, Robin and Ian returned to Ilkley, taking my golf clubs and the hickory shafted ones of David Tate given to me when I started to play golf at Hainault in 1931. Felt sad. Feel that I will never play again, although I really finished playing golf in 1939 when my handicap was 11. The odd rounds played since I’ve thoroughly enjoyed, even though I played badly.

April 29th

Jane phoned from office postponing visit until the 9th May. Helen and Robin have had a week-end break in the Lake District.

The Moores spent evening with us. Had just received news that their daughter Jennifer, aged 40, had married a New Zealander. Parents on each side not told until after the event. Moores pleased as Jennifer had been treated for manic depression. Will ‘a pair of trousers’ cure that? They are hoping so.

April 30th

A dry month, no measurable rain and finishing with cold north-east winds. Even the broad beans refuse to grow.

All my early sown onion sets have turned soft, had to plant another lot.

The buying of second hand books has revived my interest in reading. Have acquired what is for me a new method of reading, several books ‘on the go’ at the same time. At the moment Trollope’s Dr Thorne, Scott’s Peveril of the Peak, Maeterlinck’s Wisdom and Destiny and The White Cross and Dove of Pearls by an anonymous Victorian author. I read for the flavours of the period – Victorian – rather than for the story. I put down Wisdom and Destiny having just read:-

“The true sage is not he who sees, but he who, seeing the furthest, has the deepest love for mankind. He who sees without loving is only straining his eyes in the darkness”

And I pick up The White Cross etc and read this astounding opening paragraph:-

“A Sabbath on the Wolds of Yorkshire, where the rest seems eternal and the valley sleeps within the slumberous hills which encircle it like enfolding arms, while on their moory brow, tawny and princely as the brow of an Eastern monarch, glows the golden crown of the bridegroom of the heavens, who leaves it for a moment while he draws his crimson curtains around him.”

What delightful effluence [sic] verbal intoxication. My apologies to the young lady for the first word used.

And then Trollope refers to buying “a penny label to affix to a letter”.

And Scott describes Meg Merrilees as "randy", the adjective obviously having a very different meaning from the modern one.

August Courtauld in 1931 spent five months alone on the Greenland Ice Cap, the months being those of the Arctic Winter. The tent had a copy of Scott’s book Guy Mannering and in his diary Courtauld wrote:-

“Scott’s description of food in V V G”

That is how I read books now.

Nov 28th

Very cold slight snowfall.

Saw television play in which an old fashioned slate was being used. Revived very vivid memories of infants’ school days at Decoy, Newton Abbot. Slates and slate pencils were the basic materials for the teaching of writing. I can feel the squeaks of the pencils now. The slates were of 3 different kinds, those with the letters of the alphabet and lined, those with sentences e.g. “Waste not, want not” and plain ones. We graduated from the first to the third according to our proficiency. The writing was copper plate, thin up strokes and thick down strokes. A natural left-hander I have no recollection of being forced to write right handed. I suspect I was skilfully encouraged for which I salute Miss Hockaday and Miss Warcham. I can remember changing hands when my right got tired but I never mastered the “copper plate”. I know this because the best pupils had a brand new slate occasionally – a lovely black virgin surface – but my slates were always dirty grey and a light grey at that. It grieved me. Morning school started with a hands and head inspection – cleanliness – and then we had to show our rags which for most of us served a double purpose – slate and nose wiper. The rag had to be slightly damp. As it dried out it was topped up with spit. The slate was used for English, Arithmetic and Drawing – the latter a prized lesson on Friday afternoons when coloured chalks were issued. It seems so vivid I’m inhaling the chalk dust now.

Went to Ilkley on my own, Marjorie wanted to rest. Routine as usual but added my last port of call – the Craiglands. Ordered a bowl of Hyacinths for Peggy £3-45. Helen very tired, slept all the evening.

18th Dec

Planned to lunch with Helen at Harry Ramsden’s Fish and Chip Restaurant. Arrived to find a queue. Bitterly cold wind and a slight fall of snow, so bought some fish and chips and ate them at home. Spent the rest of the day by the fireside.

19th Dec

Ian and Helen finished school. My day spent between Ben Rhydding and Ilkley, resting in Betty’s, the Listers and the Craiglands. Bought Hyacinths for Jane.

21st Dec

The day to which we had been looking forward very much – our visit to Jane. Ian unable to come as Jane would be returning with us.

Left about 9.45 am, Helen driving and arrived at Pensby about mid-day. Warm welcome from Jane and Andy. Liked the house very much, had a good bargain at £18,250. V G brick built workshop – shed in the back. I’d have liked that. Luxurious new lounge suite in delicate blue – I wonder how it will stand up to ‘wear and tear’? Good bargain in teak dining suite £150 and bedroom wardrobes (2) and dressing table for £50. To me the house seemed luxuriously furnished for a young couple – and fully carpeted. Area very reminiscent of Elm Way, Ewell which I had always liked.

All went out for a drink at the Old Quay Pub, Parkgate. Got an impression of the Dee Estuary, hope to see more of it in the future.

Returned to an excellent dinner prepared by Jane, chicken followed by apple crumble – a very efficient suburban housewife and Andy is an excellent host.

Felt grateful that I had lived to see Jane in her first “nest”.

Andy was going down to Godalming to spend Christmas with his mother so Jane returned with us, Robin driving and we got home about 8 pm. We all enjoyed a thoroughly good day.

Within minutes of getting in Jane was on the phone and within half- an-hour was out meeting her ‘Ilkley tribe’.

22nd Dec

Helen starts her holiday pre Christmas preparations which she does without fuss, apparently taking it all in her stride. She is a good organiser and a good cook but I wonder if sometimes she does not think “I wish someone else was doing it for a change”.

Played chess with Jane and Ian in the evening. Enjoyed it. I wish they would play with me a bit more often.

23rd Dec

Helen had a ‘do’ at the library. Joined us in the Lister’s at 2 pm. Took Marjorie back home but I went along to the book shop. Books bought so far

A Shropshire Lad AE Housman 1936
The Virginians Thackeray 1905
The More Excellent Way Hon. Mrs Lyttleton Gell 1895
The Sea and the Jungle HM Tomlinson 1927
Lax His Book Lax of Poplar 1937
Caleb Carthew >Silas Hocking 1896
The Man on My Back E Linklater 1947
John Bright L Apjohn 1884
Ivy Silas Hocking 1894
It is Never too Late to Mend Charles Reade 1899

Saw documentary film on tele – The Irish Famine 1845-49. Felt very disturbed and sad.

Jane came in about 10.45 pm, relieved the gloom, closely followed by Ian who had been to a party at Burleigh [sic], having played squash in the afternoon. The two are in complete contrast, Jane very rarely in and Ian very rarely out except with his Mum and Dad.

We had a very arduous walk down to Ilkley in the morning, faced a very strong, gale force wind all the way. Both of us exhausted when we reached Betty’s.

Dec 24th

Helen and Robin did the final Christmas shopping. Marjorie too tired to walk down to Ilkley. Jane accompanied me part of the way, off to join her friends at the Tap. Went there after my session at the Lister’s. A very nice crowd of youngsters. A quick drink with them and then the snails progress home via Craiglands. Coming out of the Craiglands a car drew up. Jane had come to look for her grandad – very touched.

Spent the whole of the evening playing chess with Ian. The best game he has played against me. Jane out all evening with friends. Helen prepared turkey etc.

Dec 25th

Joined by Peggy at 11 am for the Christmas Ceremony around the Xmas Tree which we all enjoy every year. From Helen and Robin – the Christmas Plate, the 4th one now.

From Jane – D Attenborough’s Life on Earth

From Ian – a framed photo taken by himself at Hidcote Manor Aug. 1980

From Peggy – a bottle of whisky for me, handkerchiefs and eau de cologne for M.

Gave to Helen and Robin the Duke of Bavaria’s ? 52 piece dinner and tea set.

Gave to Jane 21 piece Doulton Tea Set Silver Birch design, sheets for 3 beds and a Good Housekeeping Book.

Gave to Ian – 4 picture albums and £5

Gave to Peggy bowl of Hyacinths

Later joined by Grace, Lily and Elsie ‘Evergreen’ friends of Peggy. Sherry for all, whisky for me.

Then Christmas Dinner. Helen has never had a failure with her Xmas dinner. We all enjoyed it, clean plates to prove it – turkey, her own Xmas pud. And Robin’s home-made dinner wine hock type, the best I’ve tasted, a varied cheese board, Stilton for me and a small glass of port. I slept soundly all the afternoon in the chair and woke up to be served with raspberry trifle – delicious.

Would have liked to have played chess in the evening or indeed any other game but Jane and Ian preferred television and Jane could not resist going out for an hour to visit her friend Nicola.

Tried to read Life on Earth but could not concentrate with Tele on. However what I did read emphasised the syntheticness of the television drama. From my odd bits of viewing I gathered that a nit wit heroine was somehow in the pilot seat of a fully laden air-liner which was out of control, and aggressive imbeciles on the ground were attempting to tell the beautiful moron how to fly the plane. What happened I do not know as I fell asleep – and so to bed.

Dec 26th

Went to Alan and Brita’s in Leeds filled with cold turkey and ham. Arrived about 4 pm to find masses of food awaiting us. Foolishly ate some and was inanimate physically for the rest of the evening. Have never seen a more self possessed, self contained and placid child than Kirsten 15 months. Watched her fascinated. An intelligent awareness which belied her placidity – remarkable. Would like to see her in 10 years time. Spoken to in English and German by Alan and Brita. No television but plenty of conversation to which I added little – enjoyed it all the more for that.

Jane had driven us down and drove us back. Car windscreen iced up when we came out. Jane drove with the confidence of youth, carrying on an animated conversation with Helen in the front seat. I was fully expecting her on straight stretches of road to jump over the back seat and chat to Laba and Grandad. Arrived home about eight. Who is for chess? I asked feebly but on went the television.

27th Dec

Last day at Ilkley, determined to do my routine in spite of feeling still full of food. Had brandy and port before going to bed and peanuts – how stupid.

A fine day, walk did me good, the haunts provide the rest and liquid refreshment. Joined by Helen, Robin and Ian in the Listers. They went on to Skipton and I to the bookshop where Dr Riley and I exchanged small bottles of whisky and then sampled them and bought Coaching Ways and Coaching Days.

Found Andy had arrived when I got back having driven up from Godalming in well under 5 hrs. He and Jane were soon busy washing, shaving, ironing and laundering themselves for Adrian’s party. We had our own cinema show with Ian doing the honours. The first part of the show was Ian’s own pictures taken this year – his first photographic year. Interesting to see, judging from the photos, how Ian takes after his Dad when younger in photographing things rather than people.

The second part – photos of the family during the years – very interesting to see the children as they developed. Helen was concerned about some of her when Ian was young. Ian paid her a lovely compliment – “you look lovely” – and I could not agree more. Very enjoyable.

Would have liked to play chess then, alas Ian opted for Tele.

28th Dec

Left Ilkley at 10.45am, Robin driving. Sunshine all the way and the Leicester Forest Service Station crowded as if it were a summer Bank Holiday. Helen then drove and back home in Bourton at 2.45 pm.

29th Dec

Helen and Robin left for Ilkley at 11 am, another Christmas over and a very enjoyable one it was.

Had a good look at the cards and letters which I find I enjoy more and more as each Xmas comes around. Sad about Winnie (81 now) mentally disturbed and unable to enjoy her two grandchildren Rebecca and Hannah, a photo of whom was sent by Jennifer.

30th Dec

Bob Sterry called in. Spent part of Christmas Day at the Cottage Hospital distributing gifts on behalf of the Rotarians.

31st Dec

Claire Clifford called in to thank us and brought some home cured ham. We gave her a slice of Helen’s Christmas Cake which we all agreed was first class.

Did not stay up to welcome in the New Year.
