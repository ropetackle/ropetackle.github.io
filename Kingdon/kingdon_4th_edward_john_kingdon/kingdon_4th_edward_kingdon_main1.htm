Edward John Kingdon
in the 1930's

Relationship: Edward John Kingdon is a Great-Grandfather of Faye, Jenny and Kate Lloyd and of Chloe and Daniel Marshall
 
Born: 1906 in Pennycomequick, Plymouth
 
Parents: James Kingdon and Elizabeth Ann Smith
 
Wife: Marjorie Tate
 
Child: Helen Marjorie
 
Died: 1982

Edward John Kingdon, known as Jack, was the only child of James Kingdon, a Fireman with the Great Western Railway, and Elizabeth Ann Smith. He was named after his mother's favourite brother, Edward John Thomas Smith, who was always known as Jack. He was born on 22nd September 1906 at 25 Holdsworth Street, Plymouth in the area known as Pennycomequick. The house was just to the north of Plymouth Railway Station and therefore convenient for his father to get to work.

In 1907 the family moved from Plymouth to Princetown on Dartmoor.

Although there is now no station at Princetown, the house they lived in is still there. It was one of a terrace of three houses, now known as the Old Station Cottages, in Station Road. The railway from Plymouth to Princetown had been opened in 1883 and the Cottages and neighbouring Station Master's house had been built by the Great Western Railway at about the same time.

The family lived in Princetown for 2 or 3 years and then, some time before 1910 or 1911, they moved to Newton Abbott, Devon where they lived at 4 Powderham Terrace. The house at Powderham Terrace is up a very steep hill and because his mother suffered from heart trouble and rarely if ever left the house, Jack had to do all the shopping for her.

Jack first went to the local school at Decoy, an area of Newton Abbott, and from there he won a Scholarship to the Grammar School in Newton Abbott. An account of his childhood and schooldays at Newton Abbott is given in an extract from his diary.

From the Grammar School he went to St Luke’s College Exeter to train to be a teacher. It was while he was at St Luke's that he met Marjorie Tate. Apparently when Marjorie first met him he had very recently hurt his leg playing football and was therefore limping badly. She thought he was crippled!

In 1928, after graduating, he started teaching at Grafton Road Mixed School, Dagenham and when Marjorie also qualified as a teacher she followed him to Dagenham.
Dagenham, which was built as part of a big slum clearance project to provide decent, modern housing for the people of the East End of London, was still being built when he started work there. In his first term at the school he had 60 children in his class, which was housed in a wooden hut. When the main school building was completed his class size was reduced to 40! The following year it became a senior boys’ school, Grafton Senior School.

Jack and Marjorie married in 1930.

During the period from October 1978 to October 1982 he wrote a diary. As well as covering everyday events at Bourton on the Water and at Ilkley during that period, the diary also contains many reminiscences of his childhood and his parents. It also contains an interesting account of his views on literacy in Victorian times and his experiences in teaching children to read.
